Project 01

Sensing Pocket

A smart pocket that senses touch and responds with light.

Sensing Pocket explores how wearable electronics can become part of an ordinary textile object. LilyPad LEDs, conductive thread, and a touch sensor create immediate visual feedback along the pocket edge, turning a familiar clothing detail into a subtle physical interface.

01

Context

The project began with a simple question: how could an everyday pocket communicate that it had been touched without using a screen, sound, or conventional control?

02

Ideation

Early concepts placed the touch area and LED pathway along the existing pocket seam so the interaction would remain familiar, discreet, and visually integrated.

Interaction

Touch anywhere along the pocket edge.

Circuit Layout

Conductive pathways follow the textile seams.

Feedback

A soft lighting sequence confirms the input.

03

Development

The pocket was assembled through iterative textile and circuit prototyping, with components tested before final integration.

04

Validation

Testing focused on whether touch was detected consistently and whether the lighting response was immediate and visible.

01 Touch input

The sensor recognizes contact along the interaction area.

02 Signal processing

The LilyPad translates input into an LED response.

03 Visual feedback

The illuminated edge confirms that touch was detected.

05

Outcome

The final prototype demonstrated how sensing, conductive materials, and light can transform a familiar textile object into a responsive interface.

Intuitive interaction

A familiar touch gesture activates the response.

E-textile integration

The conductive circuit becomes part of the construction.

Clear feedback

Light communicates that the input was recognized.

Project 02

Echo Sleeve

Translating nearby movement and distance into tactile feedback.

Echo Sleeve is a responsive wearable system that communicates spatial information through touch. An ultrasonic sensor measures proximity while an ESP32-S3 processes the input and activates changing vibration patterns.

Contribution

Interaction Design
Circuit Prototyping
Programming
Wearable Development

Toolkit

ESP32-S3
Ultrasonic Sensor
DA7280 Haptic Driver
Vibration Motor
Arduino IDE

Focus

Proximity Sensing
Haptic Interaction
Physical Computing

01

Context

The project began by exploring how information about nearby objects could be communicated without relying on a screen or sound. The challenge was to transform changing distance into feedback that could be understood through touch.

02

Ideation

I designed a direct relationship between distance and vibration. As an object moves closer to the sensor, the tactile feedback becomes faster and more noticeable.

Sense Measure nearby distance
Process Interpret the sensor input
Translate Assign a feedback pattern
Respond Deliver tactile vibration

03

Development

I assembled and programmed the electronics, connected the ultrasonic sensor to the ESP32-S3, and integrated the haptic driver and vibration motor. Breadboard testing helped me troubleshoot component communication, distance readings, and feedback behavior.

04

Validation

Testing focused on whether the system could recognize changing distances consistently and produce a clear tactile response. The demonstrations document the working relationship between sensing, processing, and vibration.

05

Outcome

Echo Sleeve resulted in a working proximity-sensing and haptic-feedback system developed for a wearable compression sleeve. The project demonstrates my ability to connect sensors, microcontrollers, and tactile outputs to create a responsive physical interaction.

Responsive sensing

Distance measurements change as nearby objects move.

Dynamic feedback

Vibration behavior communicates changing proximity.

Wearable application

The system is designed for integration into a sleeve.

Project 03

Thermal Compass Scarf

Translating ambient temperature into directional haptic cues.

Thermal Compass Scarf is a wearable concept that communicates thermal comfort through vibration rather than physical heating or cooling. A hidden temperature sensor compares the environment with a comfort range selected by the wearer and activates different haptic patterns across the scarf.

Contribution

Wearable Concept Design
Interaction Design
Haptic Pattern Design
Textile Planning

Proposed Toolkit

ESP32
TMP36 or DHT11 Sensor
LRA Vibration Motors
Haptic Drivers
Conductive Thread

Project Status

Concept Development
System Planning
Interaction Specification

01

Context

Temperature is usually communicated through numbers, icons, or alerts. This concept explores whether thermal comfort could instead be felt through a soft wearable interface that remains subtle and screen-free.

02

Ideation

The scarf acts like a thermal compass. Rather than changing the wearer’s temperature, directional vibration patterns communicate whether the surrounding environment is becoming too cold, too hot, or remains within the selected comfort range.

Too cold

Slow pulses travel from top to bottom.

Comfortable

A soft breathing pulse repeats periodically.

Too hot

Fast pulses travel from bottom to top.

03

System Design

A temperature sensor hidden inside a textile pocket would collect ambient data. The ESP32 would compare that reading with the wearer’s selected comfort range and activate a directional pattern across a band of vibration motors sewn into the scarf.

01 Sense

Measure ambient temperature.

→
02 Compare

Check the selected comfort range.

→
03 Translate

Select the appropriate haptic pattern.

→
04 Respond

Activate vibration zones across the scarf.

04

Validation Plan

Future testing would evaluate whether users can distinguish the three haptic states without looking at a screen and whether the vibration patterns remain understandable while walking, sitting, or wearing layered clothing.

01 Pattern recognition

Can users identify cold, comfort, and hot states?

02 Wearability

Do the components remain comfortable and discreet?

03 Control clarity

Can the comfort range be adjusted without confusion?

05

Concept Outcome

The project defines an interaction system for translating analog temperature data into bodily sensation. It combines textile design, environmental sensing, user-controlled thresholds, and directional haptic feedback in an expressive wearable format.

Screen-free awareness

Temperature changes are communicated through touch.

Personalized comfort

The wearer defines their preferred temperature range.

Expressive haptics

Direction and rhythm communicate different thermal states.
