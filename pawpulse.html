Veterinary wearable design

PawPulse

A pet-friendly Holter-monitoring system designed to improve comfort, electrode stability, and ECG reliability for small-breed dogs.

PawPulse combines veterinary workflow research, wearable construction, electronics prototyping, and extended real-world testing.

Role

Product Designer, Researcher, Prototype Developer

Timeline

2025–2026

Project

Individual senior design project

Methods

Research, sewing, electronics, and wear testing

Overview Challenge Research Process Testing Solution

Overview

Rethinking long-term cardiac monitoring for small dogs.

Traditional veterinary Holter setups can place a bulky recorder, long wires, adhesive electrodes, and several protective layers on a small body.

I designed PawPulse as one coordinated wearable system: a soft vest, concealed device pocket, controlled wire paths, and compact ECG electronics developed around the realities of veterinary use.

The Challenge

Current systems work, but the experience can be bulky and difficult to stabilize.

Small dogs leave little room for electrodes, wiring, a recorder, and protective wrapping. Movement can loosen placement, expose cables, and introduce artifacts into the recording.

01

Comfort

Reduce unnecessary bulk during 24–48-hour monitoring.

02

Stability

Support consistent electrode and wire placement as the dog moves.

03

Workflow

Make placement and at-home management easier for clinical teams and pet owners.

Research

Designing from veterinary experience and direct observation.

I combined expert interviews, workplace observation, precedent analysis, and hands-on testing.

A veterinary cardiologist identified electrode detachment and movement artifact as major causes of poor recordings, particularly for small, rounded, or active dogs.

The research showed that the wearable and electronics needed to be designed as one connected system.

Key insight

Fit, motion, pocket location, and wire routing directly influence signal reliability. The vest could not be treated as a separate accessory.

Design Process

From paper patterns to an integrated wearable prototype.

The project developed through repeated cycles of pattern making, sewing, electronics testing, fitting, and refinement.

01

Pattern development

I translated small-dog body measurements into a wearable pattern and evaluated coverage, openings, and adjustability.

02

Electronics testing

I tested ECG acquisition, power, leads, and signal capture before integrating the hardware into the vest.

03

System integration

Combining the components led to a concealed back pocket and more intentional wire exits and routing.

Validation

A real wear study, not only a visual mockup.

The final prototype was worn by my Chihuahua for approximately 22 hours while ECG data was recorded.

The test evaluated comfort, mobility, device retention, cable behavior, and whether the placement could produce reviewable ECG information during extended wear.

~22 hours Extended wear duration
Small breed Chihuahua proof of concept
Recorded ECG Functional output captured

Final Direction

A coordinated wearable system built around comfort and stability.

The final direction integrates the vest, electronics pocket, wires, and electrodes into one product experience.

The prototype reflects the strongest ideas from the development process while remaining honest as a functional proof of concept rather than a manufactured commercial product.

01

Soft construction

Flexible materials support comfort and natural movement.

02

Adjustable fit

The wrap structure stabilizes the wearable around a small chest.

03

Concealed pocket

Back-mounted storage protects the electronics and reduces side-hanging bulk.

04

Controlled routing

Purposeful wire exits reduce loose and exposed cable length.

Reflection

Technical function and lived experience must be designed together.

PawPulse taught me to treat materials, fit, movement, electronics, and clinical workflow as one connected system.

The most valuable part of the project was moving from assumptions into physical testing. Future development would focus on a smaller enclosed recorder, refined manufacturing patterns, testing across additional body shapes, and formal signal-quality comparison.
